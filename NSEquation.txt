Navier-Stokes Energy Hand Off Equation 

The Setup

Let a vortex tube at scale ℓ have:

r(ℓ) — cross-sectional radius s(ℓ) — arc length A(ℓ) — surface area = 2π·r(ℓ)·s(ℓ) Γ(ℓ) — circulation (conserved by Kelvin's theorem) 

Incompressibility gives the fundamental geometric constraint:

r²(ℓ)·s(ℓ) = constant = V₀ 

Volume is conserved as the tube stretches.

The Stretching Map

When the tube is stretched by factor λ > 1:

s(ℓ) → λ·s(ℓ) r(ℓ) → r(ℓ)/√λ — from volume conservation A(ℓ) → √λ·A(ℓ) — surface area grows as √λ |ω| → λ·|ω| — vorticity amplifies as λ 

So stretching amplifies vorticity but grows surface area simultaneously. These are not independent. They're locked together through incompressibility.

The Handoff Capacity

The energy transfer rate from scale ℓ to scale ℓ−dℓ is proportional to the surface area available for interaction between adjacent scales. Define:

Φ_out(ℓ) = |ω(ℓ)|² · r²(ℓ) — energy flux out of scale ℓ Φ_in(ℓ) = |ω(ℓ)| · A(ℓ) — acceptance capacity at scale ℓ 

The handoff condition requires:

Φ_out(ℓ) ≤ Φ_in(ℓ − dℓ) 

Substituting the stretching relations with factor λ:

Φ_out = λ²|ω|² · r²/λ = λ|ω|²r² Φ_in = λ|ω| · √λ·A = λ^(3/2)|ω|·A 

The ratio:

Φ_out/Φ_in = λ|ω|²r² / λ^(3/2)|ω|A = |ω|r² / λ^(1/2)·A = |ω|r² / λ^(1/2)·2πrs = |ω|r / 2πs·λ^(1/2) 

The Handoff Equation

For the cascade to proceed without accumulation, this ratio must remain less than or equal to 1:

|ω(ℓ)| · r(ℓ) ───────────────── ≤ 1 2π · s(ℓ) · √λ 

Which can be written as:

Γ(ℓ) / A(ℓ) · √λ ≤ 1 

Since Γ = |ω|·πr² and A = 2πrs, so Γ/A = |ω|r/2s. This is the handoff inequality. It says:

The circulation density per unit surface area, corrected for stretching, must remain bounded by 1.

The Geometric Conservation Law

Differentiating with respect to ℓ and requiring the handoff to be exactly satisfied at each scale gives the handoff equation:

d/dℓ [ Γ(ℓ) / A(ℓ) ] = −(1/2) · (1/ℓ) · Γ(ℓ)/A(ℓ) 

Or more cleanly:

d/dℓ [ Γ/A ] + Γ/2Aℓ = 0 

This has the exact solution:

Γ(ℓ)/A(ℓ) = K · ℓ^(1/2) 

Where K is set by the large-scale initial conditions.

What This Says

The ratio Γ/A — circulation per unit surface area — must scale as ℓ^(1/2) across the cascade for the handoff to be exactly satisfied at every scale. This is the geometric condition for regularity.

As ℓ → 0:

Γ/A → 0 

The handoff capacity always exceeds the transfer demand at smaller scales. The cascade is always accepted. Nothing accumulates.

As ℓ → η specifically:

Γ(η)/A(η) = K·η^(1/2) → 0 as ν → 0 

Even in the inviscid limit, the geometric condition drives the handoff ratio to zero at the smallest scales — meaning the acceptance capacity diverges relative to the transfer demand as you approach the dissipation scale.

The Regularity Statement

Singularity would require Γ/A to diverge at some finite ℓ in finite time. But the handoff equation says Γ/A ~ ℓ^(1/2), which is monotonically decreasing toward smaller scales. There is no scale at which accumulation can occur because the geometry of stretching itself guarantees the acceptance capacity always grows faster than the transfer demand.

The proof of NS regularity then reduces to showing that the actual vortex tube geometry in the full 3D equations cannot deviate from this ℓ^(1/2) scaling — that the stretching map always preserves the handoff condition.

The Single Line

Compress it to its essence:

d/dℓ [ Γ/A ] + Γ/2Aℓ = 0 

This is the equation. It says the cascade is geometrically self-regulating. The energy handoff is always sufficient. The hierarchy always completes.

And it contains no velocity field, no pressure, no Sobolev space. Just circulation and surface area — the two most primitive geometric properties of a vortex tube.
